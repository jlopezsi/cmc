## **CMC** Consumo, mercados y cultura

<p class="lead">
#### Universitat Autonoma de Barcelona
</p>

Somos un grupo de investigación reconocido y financiado por la Generalitat de Catalunya, 2014-SGR-502,  y financiado por el Plan Nacional de I+D+i de España en sus diversas convocatorias. Nuestra investigación es interdisciplinaria (sociología, economía, antropología, management, derecho, marketing, computación), interuniversitaria (UAB, University of Haifa (Israel), UPC, URV, University of Varsow (Polonia)) e internacional (España, Israel, Polonia, USA). Su ámbito de investigación se centra en las ciencias sociales, en especial en el estudio del consumo del arte y la cultura, y en la computación aplicada al análisis de datos qualitativos en ciencias sociales, además  promover la transferencia de tecnología de investigación.

Nuestro grupo de investigación está adscrito a la Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona, concretamente al Centre d’Estudis i de Recerca d’Humanitats i al Dept. d'empresa, aunque sus investigadores proceden de diversos departamentos (derecho, antropología y sociología, economía, management, informática) y universidades.

Nuestro propósito es investigar cómo el orden social influye en el consumo y la producción de las expresiones culturales. Epistemologícamente partimos de la premisa de que el conocimiento está socialmente construido y la sociedad jerárquicamente organizada; por ello el consumo, una actividad simbólica que nos relaciona con la sociedad y que hace visible las diferencias sociales, es de esperar que esté jerarquizado de una manera homóloga a como lo está la sociedad,  facilitando así la reproducción de las diferencias sociales. La teoría sugiere que el consumo, y también la producción,  mostrará una organización social jerarquizada de manera homóloga a como lo está la sociedad.  Este propósito de investigación requiere una visión interdisciplinaria y se concreta en estos momentos en los siguientes proyectos de investigación: 1) La estratificación de la cultura, 2) La experiencia de ocio, 3) Computater Assisted Qualitative Data Anaalysis (CAQDAS), 4) Computational Social Sciences.

Inicialmente hemos estudiado la relación entre organización social y consumo en el contexto del consumo de la cultura. Concretamente ahora estamos investigando cómo es la experiencia de consumir las expresiones culturales (arte, música clásica, música popular, cine, ópera, …). El contexto de investigación se ha ampliado a los objetos digitales, concretamente el ocio digital y los usos sociales de internet. La producción de la cultura es un proyecto relativamente nuevo y hemos iniciado la investigación de las políticas culturales del gobierno de Brasil, la cultura popular en Uruguay y la organización de la producción musical en directo en España.

## Current Projects

### The stratifiction of culture


[pereat](http://kimjongunlookingatthings.tumblr.com/)?

The stratification of leisure stream of research is based on P. Bourdieu (1979) groundbreaking monograph, *La Distiction*, published in english in 1984. Bourdieu (1979/1984) has developed a social theory of cultural practices grounded on the data he gathered about the French in the 70s. Making use of Geometrical Data Analysis methods, concretely MCA, he developed a cultural space of French lifestyles. Then he relates the  social space to the lifestyles space through the conscept of *habitus*. So individuals in priviledged social positions exibit and habit of distinction and individuals in underpriviledged social positions, one of necessity. 

However, these univorous patterns of taste and behaviors--highbrow for the privileged, popular taste for the disadvantaged--have been challenged since Peterson showed that individuals occupying privileged social positions exhibited an omnivorous pattern of musical tastes (Peterson and Simkus 1992). 

Social theories of art and culture have studied the social and cultural dimensions of consumption of cultural expressions. The numerous contributions made by Bourdieu (1979) have placed consumption, and especially the consumption of art, in a social and political context of production and reproduction of social differences. Bourdieu and co-workers (Bourdieu, Darbel and Schnapper, 2003), Peterson (1992, 1997, 2005) and US colleagues (Peterson and Simkus, 1992; Peterson and Kern, 1996) have made the pioneering contributions necessary to place the cultural habits of consumers in Western countries on the social stratification map.

Our group has contributed much of the work of drawing the corresponding map for Spain (López-Sintas and García-Álvarez, 2002a; López-Sintas and García-Álvarez 2002b; Lopez-Sintas and García-Álvarez, 2002c; López-Sintas and García -Álvarez, 2006; García-Alvarez, Kartz-Gerro and López-Sintas, 2007; López-Sintas, Garcia-Alvarez and Filimon 2008;  López-Sintas and Katz-Gerro 2005; Filimon, López-Sintas and Padrós-Reig, 2011).

Another research group has focused its attention on cultural expressions rather than society and has studied the production of meanings associated with art and culture consumption experiences. Since the pioneering work of Douglas and Isherwood (1979), who argued for and documented the role of objects in the production of meanings, interpretive investigations have studied the way in which objects are used to produce and communicate social meanings (Appadurai the two theoretical approaches that stand out in interpretive research into art and culture are semiotics and cultural sociology. 

The first uses hermeneutic methods to interpret the meanings produced in ￼contemporary consumer society (Barthes 1971; Baudrillard 1969). The second, in contrast, uses ￼￼phenomenology and ethnography to analyse the experience of individuals with objects and the way ￼in which these signify social relationships (Alexander 2008; Woodward, 2001). However, little ￼attention has been paid to the study of digital cultural expressions and their interdependence with ￼traditional cultural expressions.

Digital cultural expressions are also notably absent from interpretive research into the meanings produced during consumption. As an example, consider the articles published on digital games in 2003 in Information, Communication and Society, Issue 4, Volume 6. Of the seven original articles published, none interprets the meanings produced during the consumption experience; rather, all are interpretations of games from the production and the media side. Studies that have addressed the consumer topic have been conducted using, not an interpretive approach, but the positivist paradigm and quantitative methods.

### The leisure experience

In recent years, many of the traditional cultural expressions (music, movies, books, comic books) have become digital, sharing technologies and consumption patterns typical of the new digital cultural expressions, namely, video games, thereby merging culture and technology. The digital transformation of traditional cultural expressions and their extension to games not only has implications for the business model of the cultural industries but also for digital leisure and social uses of the digital
technologies.

While industry and governments have striven to maintain control over traditional business models (as with the draft ‘Sinde law’ in Spain), we know little of the use that the consumer makes, during leisure time, of both digital cultural expressions and digital technologies. Moreover, digital games were not deemed worthy of study until recently (Bryce and Rutter 2006) and, in Spain, were only recognised as a form of cultural expression in 2009.

Despite the economic importance of digital cultural expressions, they have been studied but little and in a segmented way, as follows: (1) the use of the information and communication technologies (ICTs); (2) the use of technology in leisure activities, recently including digital games; and (3) the consumption of cultural expressions. However, these three knowledge domains intersect when we study the consumption of digital cultural expressions during leisure time and spaces.

The purpose of this project is to study and understand the interdependence between the consumption of digital cultural expressions in different leisure spaces and the digital technologies used. We depart from two hypotheses: (1) physical and digital spaces are interconnected through the individual activities performed in both; and (2) the places where consumption activities take place have certain symbolic properties that imbue the consumption experiences in that space.
